# Zeroth Guard — background from Isaac Asimov

This note explains the **literary inspiration** behind the project name **Zeroth Guard**. It is not affiliated with the Asimov estate or any publisher; it is background for readers of the *Robot* / *Foundation* continuity.

---

## The Three Laws of Robotics

In Asimov’s fiction, positronic robots are bound by the **Three Laws of Robotics**, in order of precedence:

1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.  
2. A robot must obey orders given by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.  
3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

These laws appear throughout Asimov’s short stories and novels—not as engineering specs but as **dramatic constraints** that generate paradoxes, trade-offs, and ethical tension.

---

## The Zeroth Law

Later in the unified *Robot*–*Foundation* timeline, the robots **R. Daneel Olivaw** and **R. Giskard Reventlov** articulate a **higher**, more abstract imperative sometimes called the **Zeroth Law**:

> **A robot may not harm *humanity*, or, by inaction, allow *humanity* to come to harm.**

“Humanity” here is not the same as “a human being” in the First Law. Actions that might distress or constrain **individuals** can be weighed against outcomes for **civilization as a whole**—a source of enormous moral weight and ambiguity in the stories.

The Zeroth Law is **discovered / inferred** through reasoning and crisis, not engraved from day one; robots capable of applying it need judgment about what “harm to humanity” means over long horizons.

*(Exact wording varies slightly by edition and summary; the idea—the precedence of humanity-wide welfare over narrow interpretations of the First Law—is what matters for thematic alignment.)*

---

## Why “Zeroth Guard” for this project

**Zeroth Guard** borrows that **ordering idea**: treat **protection of people and their environments at scale** as the north star, while still operating under **explicit policies and guardrails** (the real-world analogue of “laws” that must not be clever-shopped away).

In software:

- **Autonomous defense** must not become unconstrained “hero mode”—policy, verification, and audit play the role of **stable precedence rules**.  
- **Local networks and households** are the concrete scope where harm reduction matters day to day—without pretending the system has metaphysical access to “all humanity.”

So the name is an **homage** to Asimov’s Zeroth Law theme: **protect the whole you can affect, honestly and within rules**—not a claim that this codebase implements fictional robotics ethics.
